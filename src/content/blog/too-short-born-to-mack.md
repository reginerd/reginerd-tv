## Prologue

I've been sitting on this for over a month because I keep procrastinating converting my notes into coherent thoughts. So fuck it, let's go.

Last month we took a family trip over to Freakbeat Records in Sherman Oaks. I had $20 in hand, and the desire to start building my music library back up. I picked up three CDs: TLC's CrazySexyCool, Ludacris' Chicken n Beer, and the topic today — Too $hort's Born To Mack. (I'll try to write about the other two soon — don't hold your breath though)

I grew up in The Bay during the Hyphy Era, so Too $hort has always been a household name. However, I'd never really taken the time to explore his discography beyond some of the hits. Funny enough, I learned about Blowjob Betty from a sample used in Ludacris' DTP compilation album as a kid. It was difficult for me to appreciate the roots of hip hop as a kid and teen. I often found it too basic and boring, and gravitated towards newer or more "complex" hip hop. (insert Donald Glover's stand up bit)

Over time, I've become more Unc-coded and appreciative of the genre as a whole. As I believe Scru Face Jean has said, "hip hop is a history lesson." A lot of the current rap has direct ties to older music, whether through recycled lyrics, sampled beats, or direct references. These call backs are most frequent from my favorite artists who are also Uncs — Kendrick, Clipse, Tyler. These three consistently challenge listeners to understand their roots, hip hop's history, and the significance of rap as an outlet for under-represented groups.

All that said, I decided to take it back where it all began (at a commercial level) for Too $hort. While I understand the version of Born to Mack that I own is a reissue and includes "Mack Attack" unlike the original, I'm still going to treat it as the start.

## Partytime!

Within the first three seconds of the album starting you know what time it is. I felt like I'd travelled back to a house party in the 80s. Light-washed denim, a tucked in white tee covered in a gold rope, and some Reeboks or BKs.

One of the first thoughts that came across my head was a book I've been reading to my daughter, *The Story of Rap*. The lines that immediately come to mind: "DJ's mixed beats for dancing crowds, the speakers thumped and the bass was loud. DJ's friends would make up rhymes, telling stories line by line."

"My name is short, that's so fake because I rap so long." I imagine Too $hort at one of these house parties — as soon as he grabs the mic, all the sucker emcees groan because they are never getting it back. $hort raps like he's held at gunpoint and told not to stop. Ever.

What really stuck out to me was that this album doesn't feel like a West Coast or Bay Area rap album specifically. At the time, rap hadn't really evolved geographically yet. This feels like $hort took the New York rap style and rapped about Oakland. I could hear LL Cool J spit some of these bars. The beat is the main attraction — $hort got the mic and he ain't letting that mf go. The beat breakdowns are everything. The songs are LONG and they let the music breathe.

"You Know What I Mean" is hands down my favorite track. The bassline is so sick. It feels like a video game. Growing up I never really tried to get into $hort's older stuff — I was a hyphy kid and I basically knew him from that era. Now I've got an appreciation for it as a father watching his daughter read a book about where all this came from.

I imagine $hort grabbing the mic and fighting people off by continuing to rap. He'll keep going until the beat breaks down and gives him a moment to breathe.
